NOTES TO THE POEM OF THE BROTHERS.

note i.

Page 27—line 1. “There were two springs which bub-
bled side by side.” The impressive circumstance here de-
scribed, actually took place some years ago in this country,
upon an eminence called Kidstow Pike, one of the highest
of the mountains that surround Hawes-water. The sum-
mit of the Pike was stricken by lightning; and every trace
of one of the fountains disappeared, while the other con-
tinued to flow as before.

note ii.

Page 29—line 6. “The thought of death sits easy on the
man,” &c. There is not any thing more worthy of remark
in the manners of the inhabitants of these mountains, than
the tranquillity, I might say indifference, with which they
think and talk upon the subject of death. Some of the
country church-yards, as here described, do not contain a
single tomb-stone, and most of them have a very small
number.
